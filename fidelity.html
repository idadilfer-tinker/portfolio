Context

Millions of retail investors. One search box carrying too much weight.

Fidelity is one of the world's largest investment platforms. I was embedded in the product team to redesign the core search experience — the primary entry point through which millions of retail investors discover funds, shares, and investment products.

Search sounds simple. In a regulated financial context, with users ranging from first-time ISA holders to experienced portfolio managers, it is not. The brief was to make the experience faster and more intuitive — without compromising the regulatory requirements, disclosure obligations, and risk-surfacing mandates that govern how financial products can be presented.

The Problem Space

Power users, anxious users, and compliance all at once.

The existing search experience was built for power users. It exposed raw financial data — TAR ratios, sector weightings, volatility scores — with no hierarchy and no guidance for users who didn't already know what they were looking for. The result was high bounce rates, low conversion to fund detail pages, and a steady stream of user research feedback describing the experience as "overwhelming" and "like reading a spreadsheet."

The design constraint was significant: we couldn't remove data. Regulatory requirements meant that key risk and performance disclosures had to remain visible. The challenge was progressive disclosure — showing enough to guide without overwhelming, and surfacing complexity only when the user was ready for it. Every design decision also required compliance review, which notoriously meant last-minute blockers and scope rollback.

User Mental Models
  • First-time ISA holders — 1 to 3 years
  • Unfamiliar with categories (equity, OEIC, ETF, investment trust)
  • Overwhelming raw data without hierarchy
  • Guided preference: "just tell me what to do"
  • High abandonment rate at filter stage
Platform Constraints
  • Regulatory disclosure requirements non-negotiable
  • Risk and performance data must remain visible
  • Compliance sign-off required for every component
  • No removal of data — only progressive exposure
  • Accessibility requirements (WCAG AA)

Progressive disclosure — plain language performance first, full regulated detail available on demand — combined with compliance built into the design sprint cycle, not bolted on at the end.

My Approach

Sprint-based testing, not assumption-led design.

I led a pattern of card sorting, tree testing, and moderated usability sessions — to map real user mental models against the existing UI. The data surfaced a consistent pattern: users filed in product categories ("ISA", "ETF", "Investment Trust"), not financial product nomenclature.

I produced a progressive disclosure model — animated hints cycling through real user goals — and produced a responsive disclosure model that summarises first, with regulated detail available on demand. The filter system was rebuilt from checkbox grids to guided dimension selectors, reducing cognitive load without reducing choice.

Critically, I built a design-compliance working process that integrated sign-off into the sprint cycle — co-designing the disclosure components with the compliance lead from the start, removing the last-minute blockers that had historically delayed shipping.

Process

The decisions behind the design.

01

User research to map mental models

Ran card sorting, tree testing, and moderated usability sessions to map real user mental models against the working IA — revealing consistent misfits between financial nomenclature and how retail investors actually think.

02

Goal-oriented category mapping

Identified the goal-based product category mismatch — users think in outcomes, not financial product types. Rebuilt the taxonomy around investor intent rather than asset class.

03

Goal-oriented search entry

Designed goal-based search entry with animated, task-based hints cycling through real user goals to reduce blank-state paralysis. Search leads with intent, not asset class.

04

Progressive disclosure model

Built a progressive disclosure model — plain-language summaries first, full regulated detail available on demand. Regulatory compliance maintained without overwhelming first-time users.

05

Filter system rebuilt from checkbox grids

Rebuilt the filter system from checkbox grids to guided dimension selectors — reduced cognitive load, prevented abandonment at filtering. Reduced filter abandonment rate to 35%.

06

Compliance integrated into the sprint cycle

Co-designed disclosure components with the compliance lead from the start — integrated compliance review into the sprint cycle. Removed last-minute blockers that had historically delayed shipping.

07

Moderated usability testing

Ran rounds of moderated usability testing, iterating on task completion rates and verbal protocol data to validate disclosure patterns and filter behaviour before shipping.

08

Senior product leadership sign-off

Presented research findings and phased rollout recommendations to senior product leadership, securing sign-off on the end-to-end redesign approach and compliance integration process.

What I Built

Features and systems designed across the product.

Task-based search entry Goal-oriented, animated hints — initial 35% search-to-result rate improved to 67% following redesign.
Filter system rebuild Guided dimension selectors reduced filter abandonment from 67% to 35%. No data removed — complexity hidden behind progressive disclosure.
Progressive disclosure model Plain-language summaries first, full regulated detail available on demand. WCAG AA compliant as confirmed on compliance submission.
Risk visualisation Regulated risk indicators reworked for immediate comprehension for retail investors — WCAG AA compliant as confirmed on first submission.
Fund comparison tool Side-by-side view built for post-placement, not raw data comparison.
Compliance-integrated sprint process Co-designed disclosure components with the compliance team, integrated sign-off into the sprint cycle — eliminating last-minute blockers. Rollout in 3 separate feature areas, previously estimated at 3.2 million per feature.
