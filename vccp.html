The Engagement

Three clients. One standard. Zero compromises.

At VCCP I had multiple design leadership across three regulated clients — Walby, Pension Buddy, and Cambridge Building Society — translating complex financial products and the life savings of everyday people into accessible digital experiences that met the highest standard of financial services.

Research informed every decision. I ran interviews and usability sessions to translate complex pension rules, mortgage decisions, and savings behaviour into single-question flows — proving that accessible design and conversion are not mutually exclusive. All three products met WCAG 2.1 AA.

Progressive disclosure — plain language performance first, full regulated detail available on demand — combined with compliance built into the design sprint cycle, not bolted on at the end.

The Problem Space

Each client believed their problem was unique. Most weren't.

Across all three clients, I presented a distinct version of the same core challenge — translating financial complexity into single-question flows without sacrificing compliance, trust, or conversion.

For Walby, I identified and resolved a 40% mobile bounce rate and built a product that met WCAG 2.1 AA standards for the first time. For Cambridge Building Society, I created a savings and mortgage experience that gave members clarity on their financial options. For Pension Buddy, I built a multi-platform system with clear hierarchy, components, and real-time rules that met the highest standard of financial planning experience that met their needs.

My Approach

Research-led decisions across every client simultaneously.

I structured my work around research-led decisions for each client — running separate research programmes for Walby, Pension Buddy, and Cambridge Building Society, synthesising findings against a shared research framework that allowed me to identify patterns across clients and accelerate design decisions.

Across all three products, I used a shared design language platform — a single Figma-based system — that allowed rapid delivery across clients without duplicating work or fragmenting the visual language. Research drove the decisions to show which elements, components, and patterns needed to be platform-specific and which could be shared, resulting in design decisions that made quick decisions across parallel workstreams the norm rather than the exception.

Process

The decisions behind the design.

01

Ran multi-client research programmes simultaneously

Led separate research programmes across all three clients — healthcare, pension, and financial services — while building a shared framework that surfaced cross-client patterns and accelerated decisions.

02

Adopted a savings-led approach to each product area

Structured product design around user savings behaviour to tailor each saving agreements to each user's own data needs and regulatory data requirements — ensuring no feature prompted poor or uninformed decisions.

03

Adopted single-question flows across all three platforms

Designed each platform around a single-question flow approach — accessibility-compliant, single-question decision flows that reduced cognitive load at every step across all three clients.

04

Designed adaptive components with clear regulations

Designed platform-wide component systems with regulatory adaptations built in — accessibility standards, disclosure requirements, and financial regulation baked in from the first component, not retrofitted.

05

Shared cross-client platform components and patterns

Structured a shared Figma system across all three clients — identifying components that could be shared without compromising client-specific brand standards or regulatory requirements.

06

Adapted quality assurance from other regulated organisations

Implemented a quality assurance process adapted from regulated organisations to validate design decisions against regulatory requirements, WCAG standards, and conversion data — making quick decisions at pace the norm.
